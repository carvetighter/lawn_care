# Lawn Plan — Gadsden, AL (NE Alabama, USDA Zone 7b/8a)

_Created 2026-06-23. Research-backed process aligned to the goals in `project_info.md`._

Gadsden is in Etowah County, the "transition zone" where neither warm- nor cool-season
grass is automatically perfect. Recommendations below are built around the stated goals:
healthy terra-preta-style soil, deep-rooted dark-green turf, survival in both summer and
winter extremes, natural weed/pest suppression, no pesticides, and a repeatable seasonal
process over 2–3 seasons.

---

## Getting started: 2026 prep timeline (planting spring 2027)

You won't plant until spring 2027, but prepping now sets up a far better result. The two
time-sensitive items are the **soil test** and **starting to clear the lawn** (solarization
only works in summer heat).

**Now (summer 2026 — late Jun–Aug):**
1. **Soil test this week** — cheap, and everything downstream depends on it. If it calls for
   lime, lime needs months to change pH, so testing now lets you correct it before spring.
2. **Start clearing the old lawn (time-sensitive):** solarize (clear plastic, 6–8 weeks of
   summer heat) or start sheet mulching (cardboard + 3–4" compost, breaks down over fall/winter).
3. **Measure the yard** to turn plug counts and budget into real numbers.

**Fall (Sep–Nov 2026):**
4. **Apply lime** if the test called for it — so pH is right by spring.
5. **Topdress the cleared area with compost (+ charged biochar)** to build soil over winter.
6. **Choose a seeded zoysia cultivar and find a seed source** — you're seeding, so it must be a
   *seed-available* cultivar: **Zenith** or **Compadre/Companion** (Meyer/Zeon are vegetative-only,
   not sold as seed). Look for fresh, high pure-live-seed (PLS) stock.

**Winter (Dec–Feb):**
7. **Buy seeding & watering gear** — spreader, light seed-starter mulch, and enough
   hose/sprinkler/timer capacity to keep the seedbed moist several times a day. Order the seed.
8. **Charge your biochar** (2–4 week soak) so it's ready.

**Late spring–early summer 2027 (late May–Jun):**
9. **Seed zoysia** once soil is *consistently* ~70°F+ and frost is well past — the main event.
   Zoysia seed germinates slowly (2–3+ weeks) and needs that warm soil, so it goes in later
   than plugs would.

## 1. The big fork: which grass

The goals are in mild tension. Want: deep roots + survives 100°F summers, doesn't die in
winter, dense enough to crowd out weeds without chemicals, and green. No single
transition-zone grass maxes all four.

| Factor | Zoysia (seeded — Zenith/Compadre) — recommended | Tall Fescue (turf-type) | Bermuda |
|---|---|---|---|
| Summer 100°F | Excellent | Struggles; semi-dormant, thins | Excellent |
| Winter | Brown/dormant Dec–Mar but survives | Stays green | Brown, survives |
| Crowds weeds naturally | Excellent (very dense) | Moderate; thins in summer = gaps | Good but invasive |
| Deep roots | Deep | Deep-ish, heat-stressed | Deep |
| Yearly reseeding | No — seed once to establish, then perennial | Yes — overseed every fall | No |
| Tradeoff | Brown in winter; slow to establish | Fragile when AL is hottest | Aggressive; low mow + sun |

**Recommendation: Zoysia.** Best match for these priorities — deep roots, real
heat/drought survival, and a dense canopy that smothers weeds on its own (the path to
avoiding pesticides/herbicides). Price paid: winter dormancy (straw-colored Dec–March) and
slow establishment — which actually fits the 2–3 year timeline.

**Establishment choice: seeding (not plugs/sod).** This plan seeds zoysia, which carries two
hard constraints:
1. **Cultivar must be seed-available** — **Zenith** or **Compadre/Companion** (*Zoysia
   japonica*). The premium fine-bladed types (Meyer, Zeon, Emerald) are vegetative-only
   (plugs/sod), so seeding means accepting a slightly coarser blade.
2. **Seeding is the slowest, weediest path** (details in §1b/§1c). Cheapest and least labor up
   front, but you trade that for 2–3 weeks to germinate, weeks of constant watering, and a long
   stretch of open soil that crabgrass exploits — heavier year-one hand-weeding. It also rules
   out any pre-emergent (including corn gluten) that first season, since they block zoysia seed
   too.

Choose **Tall Fescue instead only if** a green winter lawn matters more than summer
toughness and no-reseed simplicity — but expect to overseed every September and baby it
through July/August.

The rest of this plan assumes **Zoysia**, with fescue differences flagged.



### 1a. Zoysia vs. Bermuda (the two warm-season options)

Both are warm-season, both survive Gadsden summers and winters (brown/dormant Dec–March),
both are deep-rooted and drought-tolerant, and both form dense canopies that smother weeds
naturally. The differences that matter for the goals here (low-chemical, natural, low-fuss,
terra-preta soil):

| Factor | Zoysia | Bermuda |
|---|---|---|
| Fertilizer appetite | **Low** — light feeder | **Heavy** — wants lots of nitrogen to look good |
| Mowing frequency | Low (slow grower) | **High** (fast grower); looks best reel-mowed low |
| Weed crowding | Excellent — dense, stiff canopy | Excellent — but via aggressive spreading |
| Stays put / containment | **Yes** — spreads slowly, easy to contain | **No** — invades beds, borders, neighbors |
| Thatch buildup | Moderate, slow | **High** (fast growth) → more dethatching |
| Shade tolerance | Tolerates light/partial shade | **Needs full sun** (8+ hrs); thins in shade |
| Winter cold-hardiness | Slightly better; greens up later in spring | Slightly more winterkill risk in hard winters |
| Establishment speed | **Slow** (plugs/sod), pricier | **Fast** (seed/sprigs), cheap |
| Wear/traffic recovery | Good but slow | **Excellent** (recovers fast) |

**Verdict for these goals: zoysia.** The deciding factor is the no-pesticide, soil-first
philosophy — bermuda is a heavy nitrogen feeder, so getting it lush fights the low-input,
microbe-driven approach. It also needs more mowing, builds more thatch, and invades beds
(chemical-free containment becomes ongoing work). **Bermuda makes sense only if** the yard
is full sun, fast/cheap establishment matters, and more mowing is acceptable. If any part of
the yard is shaded, bermuda is out.

### 1b. Establishment cost estimates (2026, material only — DIY)

Most warm-season turf is plugged or sodded, but **zoysia *can* be seeded** if you use a
seed-available cultivar (Zenith, Compadre/Companion). Seeding is the path this plan takes —
cheapest, but slowest and weediest. Options by method:

| Method | Grass | Approx. material cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Seed** | Bermuda (common) | **$0.09–$0.18 / sq ft** | Cheapest by far; 6–10 weeks slower to mature; common-seed quality is inferior to hybrid |
| **Seed** | Zoysia (Zenith/Compadre) — *chosen* | **$0.06–$0.13 / sq ft** | Cheapest zoysia path; slow (2–3 wk germ), needs constant moisture 3–4 wks; coarser cultivars only; heavy year-one weeds |
| **Plugs** | Zoysia (or bermuda) | **$1.50–$3.50 / sq ft installed** | Spread out, fill in over 1–2 seasons — fits the 2–3 year build |
| **Sod** | Zoysia | **$0.50–$0.85 / sq ft** material; **$2.50–$4.50 / sq ft installed** | Instant lawn; most expensive |
| **Sod** | Bermuda (hybrid) | **$0.35–$0.70 / sq ft** material | Cheaper sod than zoysia (20–40% less) |

Rules of thumb: seeding is far cheaper than sod/plugs but slowest to establish. **Seeded
zoysia** is the chosen path — lowest material cost and it fits the multi-season build, but it
demands disciplined watering and year-one weeding (the open seedbed is crabgrass's best
friend). Measure your yard's square footage first to turn these into a real budget.

### 1c. DIY zoysia seeding — method & cost

Seeding is low-gear, high-patience. The make-or-break factor is **moisture**, not equipment.

**Steps:**
1. **Prep a fine, firm, weed-free seedbed** — bare soil, raked smooth, lightly firmed. Seed
   needs soil contact; it can't establish through thatch or heavy residue (relevant if you ran
   a rye cover crop — see §2b).
2. **Sow at the cultivar's rate** — roughly **1–2 lb pure live seed / 1,000 sq ft**. Zoysia
   seed is tiny; mix with sand to spread evenly, or use a drop spreader on a low setting. Don't
   bury it — rake in lightly to ~⅛".
3. **Cover lightly** with a thin seed-starter mulch or weed-free straw to hold moisture (watch
   for weed seed in straw).
4. **Keep the top inch constantly moist for 3–4 weeks** — several light waterings a day, never
   letting the surface dry. This is the hardest part and where most seedings fail.
5. **Germination is slow:** 14–21 days, sometimes 30. Full coverage takes the rest of the
   season — be patient and keep weeding the gaps.

**Cost (material, ~2,000 sq ft):** zoysia seed at ~$50–75/lb, ~2–4 lb total ≈ **$120–$300**,
plus a spreader ($20–40 if not owned), starter mulch (~$20–40), and enough hose/sprinkler/timer
capacity to water several times a day. Far cheaper than plugs ($400–$1,500) — you pay in time
and watering discipline instead.

**Timing:** seed in **late May–June**, once soil is *consistently* ~70°F+ and all frost risk is
gone, leaving a full warm season to establish before winter. Too-early or too-late seeding fails.

**Fallback — plugs:** if seeding struggles (washouts, poor germination), plugging the gaps the
following spring is the proven recovery; seeded and plugged zoysia fill in to the same lawn.

## 2. Step zero: soil test (do now)

Use the **Alabama Cooperative Extension (ACES) / Auburn Soil Testing Lab**. Pick up a soil
box at the Etowah County Extension office (or order online), pull cores from 6–8 spots,
mix, mail it to the Auburn lab. ~$7–10. Returns **pH + P, K, Mg, Ca + lime recommendation**.

**Etowah County Extension Office:**
- Address: Etowah County Courthouse, 800 Forrest Avenue, Suite 400, Gadsden, AL 35901
- Phone: (256) 547-7936
- Hours: Mon–Fri, 8:00–12:00 and 12:30–4:30 (closed 12:00–12:30 for lunch)
- Call ahead to confirm soil boxes/forms are in stock before driving over.

Critical because terra-preta-style building is pH-sensitive, and most Alabama soils are
acidic red clay. The test tells you whether to lime and what nutrients you're actually
short on instead of guessing.

**Target pH: 6.0–6.5** (both zoysia and bermuda prefer this range). If the test comes back
lower (likely for red clay), the lime recommendation will tell you how much to apply. Adjust
pH during dormancy when possible.

### 2a. Clearing an existing lawn before planting (site prep)

Whether you must kill the existing lawn depends on what's there vs. what you're planting:

- **Same grass, just thin/patchy:** No need to kill. Improve the soil, then plug or overseed
  the bare spots and let it thicken over the seasons.
- **Switching grass types (likely: fescue / common bermuda / weeds → zoysia):** Clear it first.
  - **Seed always needs bare soil** — seed can't establish against existing turf, so kill/remove first.
  - **Plugs have two paths:** (1) clear first for the fastest, cleanest fill-in, or (2) plug
    into a *weak* existing lawn and let zoysia outcompete it over 2–3 years (fits the timeline).

⚠️ **Critical:** if the existing lawn has aggressive **common bermuda**, it will smother slow
zoysia plugs — bermuda must be killed thoroughly first; don't just plug into it.

**No-pesticide kill methods (these also build soil — skip glyphosate):**

1. **Sheet mulching / smothering (best for the terra-preta goal):** Mow low, lay cardboard
   over the area, top with 3–4" of compost. In ~2–3 months the old lawn dies and breaks down
   into organic matter, feeding the soil while it clears.
2. **Solarization (best timing in summer):** Mow low, water, cover with clear plastic for
   6–8 weeks. Summer heat cooks the grass, weeds, and weed seeds. Then plant in late summer.

**Avoid tilling** — it surfaces buried weed seeds and destroys the soil structure and
microbial networks the terra-preta process depends on.

**Smother timing — important for this yard (bermuda + weeds):**

Smothering isn't instant: ~2–3 months minimum, and **longer for bermuda**, which resprouts
from underground rhizomes if the cover is too short or has gaps. Plan a **long, gap-free cover**:

- **Recommended — start fall 2026 (Sep–Nov):** Lay cardboard + 3–4" compost and leave it
  smothering all winter. Bermuda is fully killed and the bed is soil-enriched by spring, so
  you plant on schedule in 2027. Best kill plus a free winter of soil-building.
- **Workable but riskier — start late winter/early spring 2027 (Feb–Mar):** Begin as early as
  possible, give it 2–3+ months, and plant late spring/early summer 2027 (fine for warm-loving
  zoysia). Risk: a short window may not fully kill bermuda — watch for resprouts at the edges.

**Solarization is NOT required** — it's only the fast summer option and needs blazing heat, so
it's off the table outside ~June–September. Cardboard smothering works in any season; just give
it enough lead time. Overlap cardboard seams generously and extend past the lawn edges —
bermuda escapes through any gap.

**Sheet-mulch compost — volume & cost (the big site-prep line item):**

A 3–4" compost layer over ~2,000 sq ft is a large volume — budget for it up front:

| Depth | Volume needed | Bulk cost @ $30–$50/yd |
|---|---|---|
| 2" | ~12 cu yd | ~$360–$600 |
| 3" | ~18.5 cu yd | ~$555–$925 |
| 4" | ~25 cu yd | ~$750–$1,250 |

Plus a delivery fee (~$50–$100). **Buy bulk by the cubic yard, never bagged** — bagged runs
~$100–$200/yd ($4–$8 per 1–1.5 cu ft bag), which would be $2,000–$5,000+ for this job.
**Ways to trim:** go 2–3" (cardboard does the light-blocking; compost just weighs it down and
forms the seedbed), and **check Gadsden Public Works / Etowah County for free municipal
compost** before buying. DIY composting can't produce this volume in time — buy the initial
layer, then DIY future annual topdressings (the thin ¼–½" maintenance layer is far cheaper).

**Local compost sources (Gadsden area):**

  111 Burnsway Drive, Gadsden, AL 35903. Mon–Fri 7a–3p; last Sat of month 7a–12p. (256) 549-4706.
  ⚠️ This is wood **mulch**, NOT finished compost — good free organic matter for carbon/edge
  layers, but don't seed into it (coarse, ties up nitrogen). Use it to stretch the budget,
  not as the seedbed.
- **Hello Gravel** — bulk compost delivered to Gadsden (35901)/Attalla; online ordering, fast delivery.

**Smart play:** grab the **free city mulch** for the carbon/edge layers, buy **screened compost**
only for the top seedbed layer — trims the biggest cost without compromising where the grass grows.

**Suggested sequence (Option A — sheet-mulch):** sheet-mulch starting **fall 2026** → soil test +
lime/amend over winter → **seed zoysia late May–June 2027** → hand-weed through year one.

### 2b. Option B: cover-crop prep year (winter rye)

Rather than leaving the cardboard-and-compost sheet mulch bare all winter, **seed a cereal/winter
rye (*Secale cereale*) cover crop into the top compost layer**, then terminate it and seed zoysia
in summer. You keep the exact same kill method as Option A — the cardboard underneath still
smothers the old lawn — and gain a living cover crop on top. This is a green-manure / regenerative
approach and fits the terra-preta goal *better* than bare smothered ground. The one real catch is
allelopathy, now that you're seeding zoysia rather than plugging.

**Use cereal rye, not annual ryegrass.** Cereal/winter rye (*Secale cereale*) is the grain
cover crop — huge roots, terminated in spring. Annual ryegrass (*Lolium*) is a turf overseed
that lingers and fights spring green-up — not what you want here.

**You do NOT need to solarize — cardboard + compost + rye work together.** Lay cardboard on the
old lawn, top it with the 3–4" compost (same as Option A), and seed the rye *into the compost*.
The cardboard below keeps smothering the bermuda/weeds (light deprivation — it doesn't care what's
growing above it); the rye roots in the compost, then grow down into the native soil as the
cardboard rots over winter. You get the smother-kill *and* the cover crop at once — the best
version of Option B. Two things to get right:
- **Use heavy, well-overlapped cardboard** (double it over known bermuda) so it stays intact long
  enough to kill the bermuda before it decomposes — fall→spring under moist compost is plenty.
  Extend past the lawn edges; bermuda escapes through any gap.
- Solarization remains an *optional* fast summer alternative, but it's not required here — and a
  long cardboard smother is generally the stronger bermuda-killer anyway.

**Sequence (Option B):**
1. **Sep–Oct 2026** — mow the old lawn low, lay heavy overlapped cardboard, top with 3–4"
   compost, and sow cereal rye into the compost (~**4–6 lb / 2,000 sq ft**, ~$20–40).
2. **Oct 2026–Apr 2027** — cardboard smothers the bermuda/weeds below while the rye grows above:
   living roots feed soil biology, hold the clay against winter erosion, and suppress winter weeds.
   **Keep the main lawn mowed ≤12"** (city limit — see the note below); don't let it bolt tall.
3. **Apr–May 2027** — **terminate the rye** by mow/scalp + crimp, *before it sets seed* (in zone
   7b/8a it will **not** reliably winterkill). Time it off the **indicator patch** flowering, or by
   calendar (~late April) if you skip the patch. Then **rake off heavy residue** for a clean seedbed.
4. **Wait 4–6 weeks**, then **seed zoysia (late May–Jun)** — see the allelopathy note below.

**How to sow the rye into the compost (best method for this setup).** Broadcast onto the compost
and lightly cover — no seed drill or deep raking. ⚠️ **The hard rule: stay in the top inch of
compost, never pierce the cardboard** — anything that punches through (drill seeder, tiller, deep
raking) breaks the bermuda-smother layer. Loose compost is already a near-perfect seedbed, so no
tilling is needed.
1. **Level and lightly firm the compost** — rake smooth, then tamp/roll. Fresh compost is fluffy;
   firming keeps seed from sinking too deep and germinating unevenly.
2. **Broadcast in two perpendicular passes** with a cheap handheld "whirlybird" spreader (~$15–25,
   far more even than hand-casting): half the seed N–S, half E–W. Rate ~**3 lb / 1,000 sq ft**
   (≈ 6 lb for ~2,000 sq ft) — the heavier broadcast rate gives the dense stand that suppresses
   winter weeds.
3. **Cover ~½–1"** — either rake it in lightly, or scatter a thin ¼–½" compost blanket over the
   seed. Cereal rye seed is large and forgiving; both work.
4. **Firm again** (roll or walk it) for good seed-to-compost contact.
5. **Keep moist until it sprouts** — rye germinates fast (~5–10 days); after it's up it needs
   little water.

**Height limit & the indicator patch (Gadsden caps growth at 12").** A normal rye cover crop grows
2–4 ft and flowers up high — not legal here. The rye is a *cover crop, not a lawn*, so you don't
mow it to a turf height for looks; but the city cap forces two adjustments:
- **Keep the main rye mowed ≤12" all winter/spring** — cut it back toward ~4–6" whenever it nears
  the limit, leaving clippings as mulch. You lose some biomass versus a tall stand but keep the
  roots, erosion control, and weed suppression — still worth it. ⚠️ Trade-off: short, vegetative
  rye is *harder* to kill than flowering rye (it regrows from the crown), so plan to scalp + crimp,
  then re-cut or smother any green-up over the next 1–2 weeks until it's fully dead.
- **Grow a small "indicator patch" where the height rule doesn't apply** — a few plants in a large
  container or a cultivated garden bed/back corner (confirm with the city which areas are exempt).
  Let *only that patch* grow up and flower. When it shows the dangling yellow anthers (see the
  `pics/` photos), the rye is biologically ready — that's your signal to terminate the whole mowed
  area. Skipping the patch is fine too: just terminate by calendar (~late April).

**Pros:** living roots all winter (the biggest terra-preta win), erosion + crust control on bare
clay, large biomass / free organic matter when terminated, natural allelopathic weed suppression,
breaks up clay, and something green Oct–May.

**Cons / consequences:**
- ⚠️ **Allelopathy now bites you — because you're seeding.** Cereal rye releases compounds that
  suppress *small germinating seeds* for ~3–6 weeks after termination. Zoysia seed is small, so
  you **must** terminate the rye early (Apr), remove residue, and **wait 4–6 weeks before
  seeding**, or the rye will stunt zoysia germination. (This was a non-issue with plugs;
  switching to seed makes Option B and seeding slightly at odds — the buffer is how they coexist.)
- **Termination is mandatory and timing-critical** — miss it and rye sets seed and becomes its
  own weed. No-pesticide kill = mow/crimp at flowering (real labor right at planting prep).
- **Nitrogen tie-up** — decomposing rye residue temporarily locks up N; terminate younger and/or
  add a light organic N feeding so seedlings aren't starved.
- **Residue impedes tiny seedlings** — must be raked clean for a seedbed (matters more for seed
  than for plugs).
- **The cardboard — not the rye — kills the bermuda.** The rye is a cover crop, not a herbicide,
  so keep the cardboard layer heavy and gap-free and leave it down through spring; inspect the
  edges for bermuda escapes.

**Verdict:** strong choice — you get Option A's bermuda kill *plus* a living winter cover crop from
the same cardboard-and-compost, for ~$20–40 of rye seed and some spring termination work. The only
firm rule: respect the 4–6 week allelopathy buffer between terminating the rye and seeding zoysia.

## 3. "Terra preta" soil strategy (the core goal)

Real terra preta = **biochar (charcoal) + organic matter + sustained biological activity**.
Method to move red clay toward it over 2–3 years:

1. **Charge biochar before applying.** Raw biochar steals nutrients the first season (it's
   a sponge). Mix with compost and let sit/soak 2–4 weeks, or buy pre-charged turf products
   (HumiChar, CarbonizPN, BioChar DG). Most common mistake to avoid.
2. **Deliver via core aeration + topdressing.** Core-aerate, then spread a **¼–½" layer of
   compost (+ charged biochar)** so it falls into holes and onto the surface. How golf
   courses build soil. Repeat annually — carbon stays for decades as a microbial reservoir.
3. **Feed the biology, not just the grass.** Compost, occasional compost tea, and **leave
   clippings** (free nitrogen + organic matter). Healthy microbial soil is what naturally
   suppresses weeds, builds deep roots, and reduces ant/grub pressure.

Don't till it all in at once — top-down annual layering mimics nature and preserves soil
structure and fungal networks.

### 3a. Fertilizer philosophy (fertilizer is fine — pesticides/herbicides are not)

Fertilizer is *not* a pesticide or herbicide — it's compatible with this plan. Whether it
helps or hurts the soil biology comes down to **type and amount**. Guiding principle:
**feed the soil, not just the plant.**

- **Organic / slow-release = your default (helps the process):** compost, composted manure,
  biosolids (Milorganite), feather/blood/alfalfa meal, kelp. Microbes break these down and
  release nutrients slowly — building organic matter with near-zero salt. Fits terra preta.
- **Synthetic = compatible only if light and judicious:** high-salt, fast-soluble nitrogen
  stresses microbes/worms, lets the plant bypass (and weaken) the soil food web, and pushes
  soft top growth at the expense of **deep roots** — the opposite of the goal. Not banned,
  but don't lean on it.
- **Let the soil test drive it** — apply what's actually short, don't guess.
- **Zoysia is a light feeder:** ~2–3 lbs nitrogen / 1,000 sq ft *per year*, split across the
  growing season. Over-feeding zoysia causes thatch and disease.
- **Avoid "weed-and-feed"** — it hides an herbicide in the fertilizer (breaks the no-herbicide
  rule). Read labels.
- **Leave clippings** — returns ~25% of nitrogen needs for free.
- Normal fertilizer does **not** kill beneficial nematodes; just don't dump high-salt synthetic
  directly onto a fresh nematode application.

## 4. Repeatable seasonal process (Zoysia, NE Alabama)

The year-over-year cycle. Current position (late June) marked.

**Late winter (Feb–Mar)**
- Soil test every 2–3 years. Apply lime now if called for (slow to react — needs lead time).
- Sharpen mower blade. Plan the year's compost/biochar order.

**Spring green-up (Apr–May)**
- Wait until zoysia is fully green before anything aggressive.
- **Spring scalp:** once the lawn shows a green haze, mow the dormant turf down to ~50% of
  your target height to clear dead winter tissue and expose new growth; bag these clippings.
- A reel mower gives a cleaner low cut on zoysia/bermuda than a rotary.
- Light compost topdressing. First light feeding once actively growing.
- **Seed zoysia in late May–June** once soil is consistently ~70°F+ — with a full warm season
  ahead to establish. (Seed is the chosen method; plugs are only a gap-fill contingency, §1c.)

**Summer (Jun–Aug)  <-- current**
- Mow at **1.5–2" for zoysia** (fescue: 3–3.5"). Never remove >⅓ of blade per mow. Keep
  clippings on lawn. At peak growth, zoysia may need mowing every 2–3 days to stay on the ⅓ rule.
- Water **deep + infrequent**: ~1"/week including rain, in 1–2 soakings — trains deep roots.
- **Core-aerate + topdress with compost/charged biochar — once per year, late spring–early
  summer (May–Jul).** Aerate only during active growth so the grass heals the holes; May–Jul
  gives the most recovery time (important for slow zoysia). Latest safe window is late Aug.
  **Never aerate warm-season grass in fall or during dormancy** — if you miss the window,
  wait until next year.
- Spot-treat ant nests with beneficial nematodes — soil 55–85°F now, ideal.

**Fall (Sep–Oct)**
- Last feeding by early September; then stop (don't push growth into dormancy).
- Keep mowing until growth stops.
- Mulch fallen leaves into the lawn with the mower — free organic matter.
- _(Fescue path differs: this is the overseed + aerate + main feeding window.)_

**Winter (Nov–Jan)**
- Zoysia goes dormant/brown — normal, not death. Minimal mowing.
- Stay off frozen/dormant turf. Plan next year; build/turn compost.

## 5. Natural pest, ant & weed control (no pesticides)

- **Ants (incl. fire ants):** Beneficial nematodes (*Steinernema feltiae*) — soil organisms
  ants won't nest near; relocates the colony without killing anything else. Safe for kids,
  pets, bees, worms. Milky spore is a companion for grubs. Bonus: chemical fire-ant treatments
  kill 60–90% of beneficial soil organisms — the biology you're building — so nematodes do
  double duty. Application how-to in §5b.
- **Weeds:** Primary control is the dense zoysia canopy + correct mowing height. A thick,
  deep-rooted lawn leaves no room for weeds (the "mother nature" mechanism). Early years:
  hand-pull and spot-smother. Natural pre-emergent alternative to chemicals: **corn gluten
  meal** (also adds nitrogen) — apply early spring before weed seeds germinate. ⚠️ Do NOT use
  corn gluten in a year you're seeding zoysia — it blocks the zoysia seed from germinating too.
- **Grubs/harmful bugs:** Healthy microbial soil + nematodes + milky spore keep these in check.
- **Armyworms (Aug–Sept):** A real Alabama warm-season threat — chewed blades, damage worst
  early morning/evening. Natural control: **Bt (*Bacillus thuringiensis*)** spray and/or
  beneficial nematodes; may need a second pass due to egg cycles. Scout in late summer.
- **Brown patch / dollar spot fungus (humid 80s°F):** Likely in Alabama summer humidity.
  Prevent naturally instead of spraying fungicide — **water in the morning only** (never
  evening), don't over-apply nitrogen, mow at proper height, and keep airflow good. Healthy
  biological soil resists disease.

### 5a. Year-one weeding & weed ID

While young zoysia is filling in, bare ground means weeds — especially **crabgrass**. Since
you're hand-weeding year one, learn to tell them apart:

| | **Crabgrass (pull it)** | **Zoysia (the good grass)** |
|---|---|---|
| Shape/habit | Low, flat, **sprawls outward from one central point** like a star/crab; hugs the ground | **Upright, even, dense**; spreads by runners into a uniform mat |
| Color/blade | Lighter/yellow-green, coarse, wide blades; stems bend at joints | Darker green, finer/stiffer blades |
| Where/when | Mid-spring–summer in **bare/thin spots** and along driveway/walkway edges | Fills in evenly across the area |

**The key rule: pull crabgrass before it sets seed.** It's an annual that dies at frost but
drops thousands of seeds first — so remove the whole clump (get the center root) **before** the
skinny finger-like seed spikes form. A cheap weeding knife makes it quick. As the zoysia closes
in and shades the soil, crabgrass loses the open ground it needs and largely disappears on its own.

### 5b. Applying beneficial nematodes (*S. feltiae*) so they actually establish

Nematodes are **living organisms** — most failures come from killing them before they reach the
soil. The keys:

**Buy & store:** order from a supplier with good turnover (e.g., Arbico Organics); **check the
packed/expiration date.** They ship refrigerated (sponge, gel, or powder) — keep refrigerated,
use before expiration.

**Sponge vs. powder — pick by coverage, not effectiveness** (the carrier barely affects how
well they work):
- **Sponge:** smaller quantities, fresher but shorter shelf life — convenient for **spot
  treatment** (a few ant nests).
- **Powder / water-dispersible granules:** larger counts, often slightly longer shelf life —
  better and cheaper for **broadcasting** over a big area (your ~2,000 sq ft or multiple spots).

What actually drives success: **count, not carrier** (nematodes are sold by the million — buy
enough for your square footage), the right **species** (confirm *S. feltiae* or a blend that
includes it), and **alive/fresh** (in-date, kept cold).

**Apply (where success is won or lost):**
- Mix with water per the package; apply with a watering can, hose-end, or backpack sprayer.
  **Remove fine filter screens** (use mesh ≥ 0.5 mm — fine screens strain them out) and keep
  pressure low.
- **Soil temp 50–85°F.** *S. feltiae* is the cool-tolerant species, so spring and fall are ideal
  in Alabama; avoid cold or scorching soil.
- **Apply at dusk or on an overcast day — never bright midday sun.** UV light kills them fast.
- **Moisture is the #1 factor:** pre-water, apply, then water them in (~¼") to move them into the
  soil, and **keep the soil moist ~2 weeks.** Let it dry out and they die.
- **Reapply ~7–14 days later** for heavy ant/grub pressure.

**Bonus:** the moist, compost-and-biochar soil you're building is exactly the habitat nematodes
need to persist year to year — so your soil work and nematodes reinforce each other.

## 6. Realistic 3-year arc

- **Year 1 (this year):** Soil test → fix pH → establish/repair zoysia → first aeration +
  biochar topdress → dial in mowing/watering habits. Don't expect perfection.
- **Year 2:** Lawn thickens, weeds drop sharply as canopy closes, soil biology takes hold.
  Second biochar/compost layer.
- **Year 3:** Dense, deep-rooted, self-suppressing turf; soil noticeably darker and
  crumblier. Maintenance mode.
